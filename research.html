I use computer simulations to gain a insight into the physics of complex systems for which analytic solutions have so far been unavailable. With my PhD adviser Dr. A. Peter Young I have studied spin glasses, finite-size scaling in high-dimensional systems, and classical optimization problems.

In collaboration with Dr. Young and Dr. Itay Hen I have studied quantum algorithms, specifically an algorithm to solve the graph isomorphism problem using a quantum annealer.

Past projects

Spin glasses in the nonextensive regime

Classical spin systems with long-range interactions are useful models in a diverse range of topics, from magnetic materials to neural networks. Of particular interest are models in which the interactions between spins decay as an inverse power of their spatial separation. When the exponent is smaller in magnitude than the number of spatial dimensions, the free energy grows faster than the number of spins; that is, the free energy is nonextensive. The thermodynamic limit does not exist in this case.

It has been conjectured for ferromagnets [1] and more recently for spin glasses (disordered systems with competing ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic interactions) [2] that mean-field theory provides exact results not just in the infinite-range limit but also throughout the nonextensive regime.

We have shown that the transition temperatures obtained by Monte Carlo simulation agree to high precision with the predictions of mean-field theory, thus providing strong evidence for the conjectures.
